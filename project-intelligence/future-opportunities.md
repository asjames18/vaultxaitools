Every decision should move the platform closer to becoming the trusted place where people learn, build, deploy, buy, sell, and scale AI-powered systems.

## Principle

## Tier 1: High Potential, Natural Fit

These are obvious extensions of what MIT already is.

### 1. MIT Agent Registry (Public + Searchable)
**What:** A public, searchable registry of all AI agents built by the MIT community — including agents deployed outside the marketplace.
**Why it matters:** Creates the "NPM for AI agents" moment. Massive SEO surface area. Enormous community signal.
**Business case:** Freemium listing with paid "Verified" badge. Advertising to agent users. Partnership with Anthropic/OpenAI for featured placement.
**When to build:** Phase 4+, once marketplace has 100+ products

### 2. MIT Agent Hosting
**What:** Run and host AI agents for non-technical buyers. Someone buys an agent on the marketplace → MIT runs it for them for a monthly fee.
**Why it matters:** Closes the deployment gap. Most buyers want outcomes, not agents.
**Business case:** $29–$199/month per hosted agent. Revenue share with original seller.
**When to build:** Phase 5+, once marketplace is mature

### 3. Enterprise AI Talent Network
**What:** Vetted roster of MIT-certified professionals available for enterprise contract work. Think Toptal but for Black AI builders.
**Why it matters:** Enterprises struggle to find diverse AI talent. MIT's community is exactly that talent pool.
**Business case:** 15–20% placement fee on contracts. This scales the consulting arm without requiring founder-led delivery.
**When to build:** Phase 4, after 500+ certified members

### 4. MIT Certified Instructor Program
**What:** Community members can become certified instructors and sell their own courses on the Academy platform.
**Why it matters:** Scales content production without MIT creating everything. Every instructor brings their audience.
**Business case:** 30/70 revenue split (instructor gets 70%). Creates marketplace dynamics within Academy.
**When to build:** Phase 4, after certification framework is solid

### 5. Annual MIT Summit
**What:** In-person annual conference for Black AI builders — the largest gathering of Black tech entrepreneurs building with AI.
**Why it matters:** Community crystallization event. Sponsor revenue. PR magnet. Pipeline for consulting.
**Business case:** 500–1,000 attendees at $199–$499 tickets + $25K–$100K in sponsorships = $150K–$600K annual event.
**When to build:** Year 2, once community has 1,000+ active members

## Tier 2: Expansion Plays

These extend MIT into adjacent markets.

### 6. MIT for Teams (B2B SaaS)
**What:** Enterprise version of MIT — bulk team memberships, private cohorts, custom curriculum.
**Why it matters:** B2B contracts are 10–100x the value of individual subscriptions.
**Business case:** $500–$5,000/month per team. Existing consulting arm already has enterprise relationships.
**When to build:** Phase 4, after consulting arm has proven demand

### 7. HBCU & Workforce Dev Licensing
**What:** License MIT curriculum + platform to HBCUs, community colleges, and workforce development programs.
**Why it matters:** Massive reach multiplier. Creates pipeline of students who become MIT community members.
**Business case:** $25K–$100K annual licensing fee per institution. Government grant potential.
**When to build:** Phase 5, once curriculum is proven and certified

### 8. MIT Tools Affiliate Network
**What:** Become the primary affiliate partner for AI infrastructure tools (Anthropic, OpenAI, Supabase, Vercel, n8n, etc.) specifically for the Black tech audience.
**Why it matters:** High-intent audience + trusted recommendations = strong conversion rates.
**Business case:** 10–30% recurring commission on tool referrals. Could add $50K–$200K/year at scale.
**When to build:** Phase 3 — start building affiliate relationships now

### 9. MIT API & Developer Platform
**What:** Open MIT's agent registry, skill library, and marketplace via API so third-party platforms can integrate.
**Why it matters:** Platform companies that don't become platforms get disrupted by those that do.
**Business case:** API access fees. White-label licensing. Partnership revenue.
**When to build:** Year 3+

### 10. AI Accountability & Audit Service
**What:** MIT-branded service that audits AI systems for bias, transparency, and equity implications.
**Why it matters:** As AI regulation grows, companies need third-party AI auditors. MIT's cultural position makes it uniquely credible.
**Business case:** $5K–$25K per audit. Regulatory compliance driven demand.
**When to build:** Year 3+, if regulatory landscape develops

## Tier 3: Long-Range Shots

Big bets that would require significant capital and team expansion.

### 11. MIT Venture Studio
**What:** An incubator/studio that co-builds AI startups with top community members.
**Why it matters:** Creates equity upside in the companies MIT helps build.
**Business case:** 5–15% equity stakes in portfolio companies.
**When to consider:** Year 5+

### 12. MIT Investment Fund
**What:** Micro-fund ($1M–$5M) investing in AI startups founded by Black entrepreneurs.
**Why it matters:** Closes the capital gap alongside the knowledge gap.
**When to consider:** Year 5+, requires capital raise

### 13. MIT AI Browser Extension
**What:** A browser extension that MIT community members use to annotate the web with AI productivity insights.
**Why it matters:** Daily active use product. Platform distribution.
**When to consider:** Year 3+

## Ideas That Were Considered and Rejected

| Idea | Reason Rejected |
|------|----------------|
| Build a proprietary LLM | Cost prohibitive; not a differentiator; stick to application layer |
| Social network (like Twitter for AI) | Too far from core; community forums cover the need |
| Job board | Too competitive; low margin; community can serve the need informally |
| Crypto/Web3 integration | Distracts from core mission; adds complexity without clear user value |
| MIT hardware (GPUs for community) | Massive capital and ops complexity; cloud partnerships serve the need |
